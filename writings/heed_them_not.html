Heed Them Not

In the vision of the tree of life in 1 Nephi 8 in the Book of Mormon, Lehi says, "And it came to pass that I beheld others pressing forward, and they came forth and caught hold of the end of the rod of iron; and they did press forward through the mist of darkness, clinging to the rod of iron, even until they did come forth and partake of the fruit of the tree. And after they had partaken of the fruit of the tree they did cast their eyes about as if they were ashamed. And I also cast my eyes round about, and beheld, on the other side of the river of water, a great and spacious building; and it stood as it were in the air, high above the earth. And it was filled with people, both old and young, both male and female; and their manner of dress was exceedingly fine; and they were in the attitude of mocking and pointing their fingers towards those who had come at and were partaking of the fruit. And after they had tasted of the fruit they were ashamed, because of those that were scoffing at them; and they fell away into forbidden paths and were lost." But others, despite the scorn and mocking from the great and spacious building, remained at the tree and “heeded them not”. 1

And in the new testament, in John chapter 6, we read about Jesus Christ teaching the people in the synagogue powerful doctrines related to His role as their savior. However, some responded with, "This is an hard saying; who can hear it?" "From that time many of his disciples went back, and walked no more with him. Then said Jesus unto the twelve, Will ye also go away? Then Simon Peter answered him, Lord, to whom shall we go? thou hast the words of eternal life. And we believe and are sure that thou art that Christ, the Son of the living God." 2

One of the reasons I find these accounts interesting is the contrast between the groups of people involved. In both cases, the individuals involved were seeing, hearing and experiencing similar things. But some chose to stay and some chose to leave.

Each of us, as disciples of Christ, are choosing every day what kind of disciples we will be. Will we continue to be faithful, or will we wander away? Will we hold fast to what we know, or will we doubt?

In General Conference last October (October 2022), one of the talks that stood out to me the most was Are You Still Willing? by Elder Kevin W. Pearson. 3

He begins by discussing the words of the sacrament prayers, specifically the line that says, "And witness unto thee, O God, the Eternal Father, that they are willing to take upon them the name of thy Son…" He then focuses in on that word "willing".

He says, "Willingness to focus on the Savior is so crucial it is the central message of the two most quoted scriptures in the Church: the sacrament prayers. Understanding the truth of what Heavenly Father so willingly offers each of us through His Only Begotten Son should evoke our utmost efforts to always be willing in return."

Elder Pearson goes on, "If our spiritual foundation is shallow or superficial, we might be inclined to base our willingness on a social cost-benefit analysis or a personal inconvenience index. And if we embrace the narrative that the Church consists primarily of outdated or politically incorrect social policies, unrealistic personal restrictions, and time commitments, then our conclusions about willingness will be flawed. We should not expect the principle of willingness to trend positively with social media influencers or TikTok enthusiasts. The precepts of men rarely align with divine truth. The Church is a gathering place for imperfect individuals who love God and who are willing to follow the Lord Jesus Christ. That willingness is rooted in the reality that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God. This divine truth can be known only by the power of the Holy Ghost. Therefore, our willingness is directly proportionate to the amount of time we commit to be in holy places where the influence of the Holy Ghost is present."

This idea really struck me. That our willingness is directly proportionate to the amount of time we commit to be in holy places under the influence of the Holy Ghost. If we choose not to spend time under the influence of the Holy Ghost, we can’t expect to be willing to follow Christ.

I can see the truth of this principle in my life though. When I spend consistent and meaningful time in the scriptures, in the temple, in service, I feel my spiritual convictions strengthened. I feel my desire and commitment to follow God increase.

I remember a time earlier in my life when I was beginning to feel doubts about the prophet Joseph Smith because of all the worldly voices that try to tear him down. I asked my bishop for advice and he told me to just read the Book of Mormon; that was it. So I started to read the Book of Mormon every day, and I remember the Spirit strengthening my testimony and washing those doubts away. I am now much more willing to defend the prophetic call of Joseph Smith.

If our willingness is proportionate to the time we spend in holy places with the Holy Ghost, how can we increase holiness in our life? How can we create more holy spaces?

While it's essential to spend time in explicitly holy places such as the temple or the church, holiness certainly doesn't stop there. Home, work, school, the grocery store. These can all be holy places when we bring the companionship of the Holy Ghost with us, choose to act in holy ways and focus on holy things.

Elder Lawrence E. Corbridge of the Seventy taught, "Nothing in this life is of greater worth than the supernal gift of the Holy Ghost… Every good thing depends on getting and keeping the power of the Holy Ghost in our lives. Everything depends on that." 4 And as Elder Pearson is teaching us, continuing to be willing to follow the Lord Jesus Christ depends on it. It seems to me to be a pretty lousy trade to sacrifice the essential gift of the Holy Ghost for the trivialities and entertainments that the world offers. But how often do we do just that?

Elder Pearson stated, "Far too many of us live in a virtual world awash in entertainment and messaging hostile to divine identity and belief in Christ."

He says, "Are we willing to put forth more than a superficial effort into strengthening our faith in Christ? Heavenly Father loves us perfectly, but that love comes with great expectations. He expects us to willingly place the Savior at the very center of our lives."

At baptism, we make covenants to follow Jesus Christ. Later, we go to the temple to receive the endowment where we make even more serious covenants to follow Him. There, we covenant to consecrate everything we have and are to God and to His work. Everything belongs to Him. Every moment is owed to Him. Talk about unrealistic time commitments. But it's not unrealistic. It's power. Being willing to give everything to God is the path to a joyful life.

Elder D. Todd Christofferson taught, "Some see only sacrifice and limitations in obedience to the commandments of the new and everlasting covenant, but those who live the experience—who give themselves freely and unreservedly to the covenant life—find greater liberty and fulfillment. When we truly understand, we seek more commandments, not fewer. Each new law or commandment we learn and live is like one more rung or step on a ladder that enables us to climb higher and higher. Truly, the gospel life is the good life." 5

Elder Pearson agrees. He says, "Focusing on the Savior and our covenants brings lasting joy! The purpose of mortality is to prove our willingness. 'The great task of life [and the cost of discipleship] is to learn the will of the Lord and then to do it.' True discipleship leads to a fulness of joy. Are we willing to pay the price of discipleship?… Discipleship is not cheap, because the companionship of the Holy Ghost is priceless."

It reminds me of that great quote from Thomas Paine: "Heaven knows how to put a proper price upon its goods…" 6

But it is, undoubtedly, a price well worth paying.

Elder Pearson teaches, "Today, as in the times of Jesus, there will be those who will turn back, unwilling to accept the price of discipleship. As harsh and hateful criticism is increasingly leveled at the Savior's Church and those who follow Him, our discipleship will require a greater willingness to straighten and strengthen our spiritual spines and heed them not."

The mocking taunts from the great and spacious building are only getting louder and more intense. We must be strong enough and willing enough to ignore those voices and instead focus on the voice of Jesus Christ, of our Father in Heaven and of the Holy Spirit. Our spiritual survival will depend on it.

But along with the challenges of our day, we've been assured there will be many miracles. Our prophet, President Russell M. Nelson said in the April 2018 General Conference, "Our Savior and Redeemer, Jesus Christ, will perform some of His mightiest works between now and when He comes again. We will see miraculous indications that God the Father and His Son, Jesus Christ, preside over this Church in majesty and glory." 7

And earlier this month in Conference (October 2022), he said, "my dear brothers and sisters, so many wonderful things are ahead. In coming days, we will see the greatest manifestations of the Savior’s power that the world has ever seen. Between now and the time He returns 'with power and great glory,' He will bestow countless privileges, blessings, and miracles upon the faithful." 8

These may be challenging times, but they will also be very exciting, inspiring and glorious if we we will stand in the right places and do the right things. If we will be the faithful that President Nelson is talking about.

I know that we are children of loving heavenly parents. I know that Christ lives and that He is our savior. I know this is Christ's church and it is led by living prophets. I know that the Book of Mormon is the word of God. I have plenty of weaknesses and shortcomings, plenty of ways I wish I was better, but I know these things are true and I will never leave them. And I know that if I am willing to do that, Christ will make up for all my deficiencies and bring me home safe. I pray that we may all know these things, continue to know them and experience the joys that the gospel has to offer. Now and forever.

Originally written in October 2022 for a sacrament meeting talk in the Logan 12th Ward in Logan, Utah.
